My research focuses on the intersection between economic competition, complex systems, and the law. In particular, I'm interested in how the structure of markets and the actions of individual agents combine to create emergent behaviour.

Ongoing projects

Computing Competition with Agent-Based Models (2021, in-progress)

This paper applies techniques from complexity science to competition law. I build an agent-based model to simulate zero-price markets. Using the model, I investigate the dynamics of these markets, and the effect of policy remedies. The model is able to reproduce stylized facts about zero-price markets.

Publications

Zero price, Zero Competition: How Marketization Fixes Anticompetitive Tying in Monetized Markets (2021, forthcoming) - Todd Davies & Zlatina Georgieva

This paper outlines why monetized (zero-priced) markets often tip towards a single dominant firm, and outlines a novel regulatory remedy by which such market failures can be prevented. This paper features in the 2021 conference for the Academic Society of Competition Law.

Reconceiving Big Tech: Decentralizing the Industry to Align Incentives and Maximize Total Welfare (2021, Concurrences) - Todd Davies

Winner of the Concurrences 2020 Best Student Paper award. This paper is essentially an earlier version of the paper above, and describes a structural approach to understanding market power online.
